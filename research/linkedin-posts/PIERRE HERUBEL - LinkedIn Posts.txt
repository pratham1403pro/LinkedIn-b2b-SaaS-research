Date: 16/06/2026

I've published B2B educational content since 2022.
A lot of brands starting content in 2026 make this mistake:

1. They assume their audience logs in to social media to consume complex content.
They think "we need to show we're the experts" so they create content too dense to be discoverable. The content would fit a blog or a white paper, but not social zero-click content.
They forget their audience's behaviors:
- People check their feed between meetings;
- They open one tab to search for specific info;
- They post their own content, and scroll a bit.

But some B2B brands make the exact opposite mistake:

2. They assume their audience wants only broad content like stories.
They think "people don't have enough attention for technical content" so they create content that lacks depth, unique insights, or expertise.
But the truth is that B2B professionals:
- Want to get a unique POV and narrative;
- Want content that makes their career grow;
- Want actionable insights to fix key problems.

***

Marketer: "Well, what type of content should we create then?"
Me: "You need to organize your content with 3 levels of complexity"

You can publish about one topic and layer the information across complexity levels:
1. Accessible (but unique) content
2. Intermediate content to show more depth
3. Advanced content for the "Aha! moments"

I've been doing this for 4 years and it's working well.
It works thanks to the halo effect.
Your audience reading a level 1 piece of content will think "if this is good, the other pieces must be good too" and then deep dive into your expertise.

Date: 19/06/2026

CEO: "That's our marketing plan across 7 channels"
Me: "So who in your team is going to do this?"
CEO:
"We have 2 marketers in house"

Let's address the elephant in the room:
Your strategy doesn't fit your marketing budget.

Because:
- Each marketing channel requires time and focus.
- Every asset you produce needs a real commitment.
- Your team also needs time for analysis and iteration.

So if you split your team's time too much, they won't be able to create high quality marketing assets (content, ads, emails...)

Instead opt for:
- A realistic and focused strategy
- Built on the resources you already have
- Less time spent in meetings, more time creating

POST 3

URL: https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7473383049976631296/

Date: 18/06/2026

- "Posting daily" is NOT a strategy:
- "Launching ads" is NOT a strategy;
- "Doing webinars" is NOT a strategy;

Most founders confuse strategy with tactics.
Because of this confusion, they lack a high level plan.
- Who are we helping;
- For what problem;
- How we solve it differently;
- And what do we say.

As a result, they get easily distracted:
- "Let's try this new channel!"
- "I read about a new automation hack!"
- "We don't want to miss this opportunity"

And this lack of focus puts the business into a reactive posture:
No clear direction → A new trend happens → Reaction → "Let's try this new tactic!" → Intensify the lack of direction

To get out of this reactive posture, founders need to distinguish strategy from tactics:
1. Strategy comes first: It's the high-level plan that guides how to achieve revenue goals.
2. Tactics come second: It's the specific actions and methods used to execute the strategy.

Now here's a last important note:
The feedback loop between tactics and strategy is crucial.
Tactics' results always inform the strategic decisions.
